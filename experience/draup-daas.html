Draup · SDE 2 · Dec 2024 — Present

DAAS

GraphQL · Airflow · Trino

I owned a data-delivery framework that lets customers get exports on a schedule, wired through GraphQL and executed end-to-end in Airflow—from gathering requirements and calling our graph layer to running Trino SQL. Later I layered in agent-assisted SQL optimization, which cut average query time by about a fifth and dropped failures substantially.

The team needed a repeatable way to stand up customer-specific data deliveries without hand-built jobs for every account. I built a framework that automates those pipelines behind a GraphQL API: schedules, configuration, and orchestration live in one place, and operators can reason about deliveries as structured workflows rather than ad hoc scripts.

Airflow is the backbone of the run path—collecting requirements from metadata, invoking the graph API where needed, and executing the generated Trino SQL against our warehouse. That end-to-end contract made failures easier to trace and retry, and kept the surface area for new customer shapes small.

The largest step-change came from augmenting the framework with AI agents that review and refine generated SQL before execution. That reduced bad or inefficient queries hitting the cluster, improved typical runtimes by roughly 20%, and cut the failure rate on automated runs by about 40%, which mattered a lot once delivery volume grew.
